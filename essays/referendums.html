Against Referendums

This was written in July 2020, prior to the November 2020 general election and referendum.

When New Zealanders vote in this year’s general election, we will also participate in a world-leading double referendum. One referendum question will ask about the legalisation of cannabis for recreational use, and the other will ask whether euthanasia should be legalised in certain circumstances.

Although there is ample debate surrounding the content of these bills, little has been said about whether a referendum is the right way to make these political decisions. One of the few voices on the issue has been Dr Bryce Edwards, a senior associate at the Institute for Governance and Policy Studies at Victoria University in Wellington.

Edwards says:

“Referendums should be celebrated for resolving difficult and divisive issues, as well as for their ability to draw the public into political decision-making and debate. Putting questions to the public means that a much bigger societal debate occurs, and ultimately the decisions made have greater legitimately [sic] and endurance.”

Are referendums the best way to resolve political issues? Are they, as Edwards says, "even more legitimate than typical parliamentary process"?

What is the role of politics?

The role of politics is not merely to legislate and distribute resources. If it were, we could defer to experts. Politics exists to reconcile the conflicting desires and aspirations of people within a polity, and to do so without violence through reasoned and respectful debate.

A political resolution, then, is not merely formal decisions on a course of action. The role of a political resolution is, more fundamentally, the reconciliation of diverging or opposing views. This is something a referendum does not achieve, yet it is essential to the maintenance of a well-functioning democracy.

In a democracy, differences of opinion are overcome by a common acceptance of the legitimacy of its decision-making processes—not necessarily of the decisions themselves but of the method of making them.

Former UK Supreme Court Justice, Jonathan Sumption, has said that although majority-rule is the basic principle of democracy, this only means that a majority suffices to authorise the State’s acts:

'It is not enough to make them legitimate, because majority-rule is no more than a rule of decision. It does nothing to accommodate our differences; it simply restates them in numerical terms. A legitimate democracy cannot operate on the basis that a bare majority takes one hundred percent of the political spoils, without any regard for the minority. If it did, it would harbour large and permanently disaffected groups in its midst who had no common bonds to transcend their differences with the majority.’

If the majority can do whatever it wants, without regard for the minority, then the ethic of reciprocity—the basis of legitimate democratic government—is threatened. That is why all democracies have evolved methods of diluting the power of majorities. One such method is representative politics.

The General Good

Today we could, in theory, abolish representative politics. For the first time since the whole citizenry of ancient Athens gathered in the Agora to transact public business, it would be technically feasible for the electorate to vote directly on every measure. In fact, no democracy works like that. They act through elected legislatures. They do this not only for reasons of practicality, but also on principle.

In one of his contributions to the federalist papers, James Madison gave what is still the classic justification for representative politics: A chosen body of citizens is less likely to sacrifice the true interests of the country to short-term considerations, unthinking impulses or sectional interests. “Under such a regulation”, he wrote, “it may well happen that the public voice pronounced by the representatives of the people will be more consonant to the public good than if pronounced by the people themselves.”

In England, Madison’s contemporary, the politician and philosopher Edmund Burke, carried this idea further. 'Parliament,' he said, 'is not a congress of ambassadors. . . Parliament is a deliberative assembly of one nation with one interest—that of the whole: where, not local purposes, not local prejudices ought to guide, but the general good resulting from the general reason of the whole.'

Members of parliament were there to represent the national interest and not the opinions of their constituents. This view might be called elitist, but political elites have their uses. Professional politicians should be expected to bring to their work a more reflective approach, a broader outlook, and a lot more information than their electors. There is a further point. Nations have collective interests which extend over a longer time scale and a wider geographical range than are ever likely to be reflected in the public opinion of the moment. Today, for example, we face issues such as climate change, on which the interests of future generations differ radically from those of the current electorate. In the 18th century, David Hume pointed out what he called the incurable narrowness of soul that makes people prefer the immediate to the remote. If we are to avoid the same narrowness of soul, we must take a view of the national interest which transcends snapshots of the current state of electoral opinion.

The Pitfalls of Referendums

A referendum is a device for bypassing the representative political process. It takes decision-making out of the hands of politicians, whose interest is generally to accommodate the widest possible range of opinion, and places it in the hands of individual electors who have no reason to consider anything but their own opinion. Consequently, a referendum not only inhibits an independent assessment of the national interest by professional politicians, but also promotes narrowness and personalisation of decisions which have implications beyond the individuals whose opinion decides them.

In an age of increasing suspicion of elites, as well as a growing public discontent with democracy, some have called for more devolution of power to voters on key issues. However, an important object of modern democratic constitutions is to treat the people as a source of legitimacy while placing barriers between them and the direct operation of the levers of power. This is done to contain the fissiparous tendencies of democracy—to counter the inherent tendency of democracy to destroy itself when majorities become a source of instability and oppression.
