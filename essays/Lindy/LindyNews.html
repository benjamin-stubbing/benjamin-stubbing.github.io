Plane Thoughts: A Newspaper Called Lindy

'You should have this thought ready in hand against any eventuality: 'I have seen this before.' Generally whenever you look you will find the same things. The histories—ancient, more recent, and modern—are full of them: cities and households are full of them today. There is nothing new. All is familiar and all short lived.'

—Marcus Aurelius, Meditations 7.1
'Look back over the past—all those many changes of dynasties. And you can forsee the future too: it will be completely alike, incapable of devitiating from the rhythm of the present.'

—Marcus Aurelius, Meditations 7.49

While seconded to Christchurch, I haven’t devoted my typically excessive amount of cognitive surplus to reading the news. Yet I haven’t missed anything and I’ve enjoyed myself. Reflecting on this, I realise—as I seasonally do—I should read less news.

The Lindy Effect is one way of thinking about why some things endure. The Lindy Effect says that the future life expectancy of some non-perishable things, like technology or ideas, is proportional to their current age, so that every additional period of survival implies a longer remaining life expectancy. Where the Lindy Effect applies, mortality rate decreases with time.

Macbeth was written around 1606 but its popularity endures. Periodically, some self-described educational specialist makes a case for the revival of Shakespeare, but Shakespeare doesn’t need rejuvenation or defence. Macbeth has endured because there is something in it that is resilient to changes in fashion.

News is not Lindy by definition. It doesn’t last and, what’s more, it doesn’t generate value unless your value-add is to know the news. It’s like day trading, which is a sub-optimal strategy for value creation unless it's your vocation and you have access to the fastest internet and private offerings. Day trading is fine if it’s your job to day trade. Similarly, if you’re a professional policy wonk you should know the latest policy news.

If the goal, however, is to create value (for personal pleasure or professional diligence) by separating the signal from the noise, then we must take a wider view. The noisiness of political news is well illustrated by Andrew Chen’s beautiful graph of political poll results over time:

Political news is inherently fuzzy and genuine changes are occasional. If you’re too focused on a single tree, you won’t see the forest.

As well as being noisy, a lot of news (read: punditry) is reliably misleading. Philip Tetlock’s research has demonstrated that “expert” predictions are often less accurate than monkeys throwing darts at a board. For a great contemporary example of the abysmal track record of pundits, Jerusalem Demsas has done a wonderful write up on some of the worst predictions made by economists apropos of COVID-19.

When I return to reading the news, I'm going to adopt a sort of dollar-cost averaging approach. In financial investing, dollar cost averaging is an investment strategy where you invest a fixed amount at fixed intervals—say, 10% of your pay check every month. By doing this, you buy more when the market is down (the cost of assets is lower) and less when the market is up (the cost of assets is higher). Similarly, when reading the news, I’ll limit the amount of investment (time) I place in reading news. Busy news days are like busy trading days: it is the habbit of the market to ovevalue stocks which have shown excellent growth or are glamorous for some other reason. We can expect the market to undervalue what isn't fashionable. So, we should drop what is fashionable and instead read what is timeless to find value that is untemporamental.
