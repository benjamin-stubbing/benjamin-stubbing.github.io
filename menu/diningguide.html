Wellington Dining Guide

I like food. My friend Rudy and I blog about restaurants and eateries in Wellington. You can follow us on Twitter.

Recommendations welcome!

For my part, I've been inspired by this book and this blog by Tyler Cowen. I'd like Wellington Dining Guide (our blog) to be Wellington's answer to Tyler's Ethnic Dining Guide. It's not the same though—it couldn't be if we tried. We bring our own context.

Here's something Rudy and I wrote about our ethos:

We are two people who like food. We watched an embarrassing number of food shows as children, especially Anthony Bourdain and Rick Stein. We whakapapa to whānau who appreciate their kai. Rudy’s father was a sous-chef raised in the kitchen of his parent’s pub. Benjamin’s dad is a home cook hero with a special love for making pizza in the woodfire oven he built.

We are two recent graduates. Rudy studied history and Benjamin studied economics. History and economics are useful tools for finding and appreciating good food. As Tyler Cowen has written, food is a product of economic supply and demand. To find good food, go where the supply is fresh, the suppliers are innovative, and the demanders are informed. One might say that everything else is commentary—but commentary is precisely what people look for in food writing and recommendations. Economics provides a topical, tactical sort of intel on food—the sort that allows a person to see the different angles but somehow entirely misses the point. While it’s helpful to have a comprehensive tactical knowledge of where the flour, vegetables, meat, spices, and financial capital that forms the foundation of your favourite, the sublime quality of eating well evades the grasp of this analysis. The historian's approach to storytelling casts a light on what is missing.

We are also two people who love Wellington. It is a familiar love, like an old married couple. Familiarity breeds comfort and contempt in equal measures. It's a tired thing to say, but Wellington was once called "the coolest little capital in the world" by Lonely Planet. Something about that phrase reminds me of Jeff Daniel's iconic monologue in the pilot episode for Newsroom. To borrow Daniel's point, Wellington might not be that cool anymore, but it can be—and there are pockets of excellence worth celebrating.

This guide aims to help you enjoy good food in Wellington and keep our favourite places in business. Effective consumer choice improves your eating and, in the long run, improves the quality of available restaurants. This guide is also intended to revive our romance with our old sweetheart. What better way than by taking her out for dinner?
